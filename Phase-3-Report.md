# Phase 3 Deliverables

## 1. Phase 3 Report:
* Fabrication details (filament type, printer, settings, number of reprints)
  -
### Printers Used
The team utilized an **Elegoo Centauri Carbon**, a **Bambu X2D**, and initially a **Prusa XL**.
* **Elegoo Centauri Carbon:** Required for the PPS-CF parts as it could reach the necessary high nozzle temperatures (320°C).
* **Bambu X2D:** Used for the PETG components (max temperature 300°C).
* **Prusa XL:** Used initially for larger parts before access was lost.

### Filaments & Settings
* **PETG:** Used for primary structural components to maximize impact resistance. Printed using standard Polymaker profile settings.
* **PPS-CF (Carbon Fiber reinforced):** Used for high-stress rotational and shear components. 
    * **Nozzle Temperature:** 320°C
    * **Bed Temperature:** 85°C
    * **Print Speed:** Slowed from 250mm/s to 100mm/s to ensure proper layer adhesion.

### Infill
Initial prints were set to 100% infill for maximum density. However, during reprints, the PETG infill was reduced to 50% to conserve limited material while still maximizing structural strength.

### Reprints
A total of 3 sets of reprints were required. Notably, the bicep piece was originally printed as two parts on the larger Prusa XL, but after losing access to that printer, it had to be split into three separate parts to fit the smaller build volumes of the remaining printers.

* Detailed discussion of failures, mistakes, and surprises
   -
**   Throughout Phase 3, there was a handful of issues that arose during fabrication, assembly, and testing that were highlighted in the current design. The mechanism in concept was able to function as intended, though many of the failures originated from manufacturing and material limitations. 
**   The pulley mechanism lacked a retention mechanism that inturn caused it to slide loosely on its intended axle. It was able to function as intended but created a risk of pulley detachment mid operation. 
**   The gear rack had an issue were the lip was too small relative to the printer resolution, directly resulting in less than desirable quality. Additionally the teeth were to shallow to properly engage and lock the mechanism in place, causing slippage and unreliable tension control. Additionally, there were several clearence/interferance issues. One of them being was the gear rack lip had obstructed the desired spring placement and the pawl was too high up close to the gear rack mechanism, resulting in it being difficult to install the spring in the desired position. 
**   The most critical issue occured in the archer release mechanism in which the axle experienced high stress under loading and began to fracture. This is a direct result to the material limitations of this printed part. As a result of this installment, misalignment and potential premature releasing of the locking mechanism could result in the loss of controlled tension. 
**   The last issue was the release block that would disengage the archer release mechanism and release the tension. The usage of the small block required awkward access or a second has which is less than ideal when the system is under a load. 
** Overall, the observed failures were primarily due to the limitations in material strength, print resolution, improper tolerance, and at times poor user accessibility rather than flaws in the core concept. 
    
 * Concrete list of design changes you would implement in a second iteration
    -
    - Based on the issues discussed in the previous segment, several design improvements would be instated in a second iteration to ensure user accessbility, reliability, and manufacturability. The pulley system would under go a redesign to correct the retention mechanism. It could involved the addition of shaft collars, clips, or a shouldered axle to prevent the wheels' laterval movement and to reinforce its stationary design. The gear rack would be modified to counter the 3D printing constraints. The teeth would be increased to enhance its structural integrity, mechanical engagement, and print resolution. The rendition of larger and deeper teeth would overall minimalize slippage and permit more reliable tension adjustments. For the clearance and tolerance issues, they would be refined throughout the design. Additional space would be emphasized for the sake of incorporating the spring. Additionally, the gear rack lip would be either redesigned or repositioned to improve that the overall mechanism is properly fitted to reduce any potential post processing issues. The archer release mechanism would be reinforced to handle higher loads of stress. The axle's material would be replaced with a metal component and/or the diameter would be increased to reduce stress concentrations. A combination of these two methods would allow the system to have more improved durability. Along side this change, the locking and release interface would also be improved upon to eliminate the premature release of the pin. In whole, the archer release mechanism would be reworked with an emphasis on ergonomics. A more accessible solution would be a trigger, lever, or cable actuated system that would be located on the hand or wrist. Resultingly, this would allow the user to safely release the mechanism under load without having to experience awkward positioning.
